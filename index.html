About Me

"I don't study behavior — I live inside it. Fourteen years of managing ADHD taught me to reverse-engineer every system until I found where the friction was. That instinct became a career."

I'm a Behavioral Product Strategist and UX Researcher who works at the intersection of cognitive science, behavioral economics, and product design. My practice is built on a single conviction: that most product failures aren't feature failures — they're behavioral failures. The interface shipped, but the behavior never changed.

Growing up with ADHD shaped how I see design. I became a first-hand expert in friction — the invisible cost that stops people before they even know they've stopped. That personal archive became professional currency. I developed a sensitivity to where systems break for the people systems weren't originally designed for, and how fixing those breaks — what researchers call the Curb Cut Effect — often produces the most elegant, universally usable solutions.

Across fintech, edtech, SaaS, and healthcare, I've led behavioral audits, rebuilt research-to-roadmap pipelines, and translated cognitive bias theory into testable product decisions. I don't hand off decks and disappear. I stay in the room until the behavior actually changes — in the product, and on the metrics dashboard.

§ II

My Framework

Every engagement runs through a three-stage behavioral operating system.
Observation before diagnosis. Diagnosis before intervention.

01 — Behavioral Audit / Where is friction hiding?

I map every decision point in the user journey against a library of cognitive biases, heuristics, and known failure modes. This isn't a standard usability audit — it's a behavioral forensics exercise. I'm looking for the gap between what users intend to do and what the system lets them do, and specifically where that gap is caused by cognitive architecture rather than feature absence.

↓    surface-level symptoms resolved into root behavioral causes

02 — Research Synthesis / What is the evidence structure?

Behavioral hypotheses need evidence. I triangulate qualitative research (interviews, contextual inquiry, diary studies) with quantitative signals (funnel data, event logs, cohort retention) to build a ranked diagnosis — prioritized not by stakeholder instinct but by behavioral impact and confidence level. This is the layer that prevents teams from shipping interventions that treat the wrong thing.

↓    evidence converted into ranked, testable behavioral hypotheses

03 — Intervention Design / What changes the behavior?

Interventions are designed at three levels: structural (information architecture, flow redesign), contextual (defaults, framing, timing), and motivational (progress mechanics, social proof, identity alignment). Each intervention is testable, tied to a behavioral metric, and designed to produce a measurable shift — not a more polished interface, but a different decision outcome.

Case File · 01 · Fintech / Investment Platform ▲ 34% Activation · ▲ 22% 30-Day Retention

The Paralysis Problem — Rebuilding a Broken Investment Onboarding Flow

Scope: Full Behavioral Audit + UX Research + Intervention Design  ·  Duration: 14 weeks  ·  Team: Solo Research Lead

The Problem

An investment platform was seeing 61% drop-off during onboarding — specifically at the "risk profile" and "first investment" steps. PM hypothesis: users didn't understand the product. Behavioral hypothesis: they understood it fine, but the decision architecture was triggering choice paralysis and loss aversion simultaneously.

Research Methods

12 moderated think-aloud sessions, funnel analysis across 3 cohorts, eye-tracking prototype testing, 6 expert behavioral walkthroughs. Primary question: at which decision point does the cognitive cost exceed the user's perceived reward?

Behavioral Diagnoses

Choice Overload

14 investment categories presented simultaneously with no progressive disclosure. Working memory saturated before commitment.

Loss Aversion Amplification

Risk questionnaire used loss-framed language ("What if your portfolio drops 30%?") as the first substantive user interaction.

Identity–Action Gap

No behavioral anchoring to user's stated financial goals. Product felt transactional, not aligned with self-concept as an investor.

Interventions Designed

Progressive disclosure architecture: Reduced initial choice set from 14 to 3 goal-aligned paths. Full optionality available post-commitment.

Gain-framed resequencing: Moved risk questionnaire post-goal anchoring. Rewrote all risk language to reference aspirational outcomes before potential losses.

Commitment escalation: Introduced a ₹100 "first move" mechanic — low-stakes first investment to activate identity as investor before asking for larger commitment.

Measured Impact (90-day post-launch)

+34% onboarding activation  ·  +22% 30-day retention  ·  Average first investment increased ₹1,200 → ₹4,800  ·  Support tickets about "how to start" dropped 58%

Case File · 02 · Healthcare / Patient Engagement ▲ 41% Medication Adherence · ▲ 28% Follow-Up Completion

The Invisible Wall — Redesigning Medication Adherence for Chronic Disease Patients

Scope: Behavioral Research + Intervention Design + Pilot Testing  ·  Duration: 18 weeks  ·  Team: Research Lead + 1 Clinical Advisor

The Problem

A digital health platform for chronic disease management had a 38% 3-month medication adherence rate. Clinical team attributed it to patient motivation. Behavioral audit revealed a fundamentally different picture: the friction wasn't motivational — it was cognitive and contextual.

Research Methods

Diary study across 22 patients over 4 weeks, in-context behavioral observation, 8 clinician interviews, behavioral pattern analysis of 2,400 app sessions. Key lens: where does the intended behavior break down at the moment of execution?

Behavioral Diagnoses

Present Bias Collapse

Long-term health framing ("protect your future health") failed to compete with immediate pain of side effects and routine disruption.

Habit Loop Absence

No behavioral anchor connecting medication to existing daily routines. Reminders felt like interruptions, not cues.

Autonomy Erosion

App communicated as a compliance tool ("You missed your dose"). Generated psychological reactance in independent patients.

Interventions Designed

Behavioral anchoring protocol: Co-designed personalized habit stacks with patients — linking medication to existing high-consistency behaviors (morning coffee, bedtime routine).

Near-horizon reframing: Shifted all in-app messaging from long-term health outcomes to 7-day progress windows. Weekly streaks and pattern visibility instead of annual health projections.

Autonomy-supportive language redesign: Rewrote all notification and feedback copy from compliance framing to agency framing ("You kept your streak going" vs. "Reminder: Take medication").

Measured Impact (Pilot: 90 days, 180 patients)

+41% medication adherence rate  ·  +28% follow-up appointment completion  ·  Patient-reported friction score dropped 44%  ·  6-month retention in program increased 33%

Case File · 03 · EdTech / Learning Platform ▲ 47% Course Completion · ▲ 31% Weekly Active Learners

The Completion Illusion — Fixing a Learning Platform's Engagement Architecture

Scope: Behavioral Research + Learning Experience Redesign  ·  Duration: 16 weeks  ·  Team: Lead Researcher + 2 Designers

The Problem

A professional upskilling platform had 73% enrollment but 11% course completion. Content quality reviews were strong. The product team concluded learners lacked discipline. The behavioral audit concluded the product was architecturally designed to feel complete before it was.

Research Methods

Mixed-methods: 16 learner interviews, cohort retention analysis, session recording review (240 hours), learning diary study with 30 participants over 3 weeks. Focus: the specific moment and behavioral trigger of disengagement.

Behavioral Diagnoses

Completion Illusion

Progress bars showed module-level completion, triggering false closure. Learners felt "done" after completing a section, not the course.

Effort Discontinuity

No bridging between sessions. Each login required re-establishing context — cognitive restart cost accumulated until learners stopped returning.

Social Isolation

Learning was entirely private. No normative data, peer visibility, or identity signal from being a learner in progress.

Interventions Designed

Journey-level progress redesign: Replaced module completion bars with journey maps showing distance traveled and remaining path. No partial-completion satisfaction signals.

Session continuity hooks: "Where you left off" contextual summary at login, reducing re-orientation cost and establishing behavioral momentum before the first click.

Social learning integration: Cohort progress visibility ("14 learners in your batch are on Module 4") and opt-in peer accountability pairings.

Measured Impact (60-day post-launch)

+47% course completion rate  ·  +31% weekly active learners  ·  Average session length +19 minutes  ·  NPS improved from 34 → 61

Case File · 04 · SaaS / B2B Productivity Tool ▲ 38% Feature Activation · ▲ 19% Expansion Revenue

The Phantom Feature Problem — Driving Adoption of High-Value Capabilities Nobody Used

Scope: Behavioral Audit + Feature Discovery Research + Activation Redesign  ·  Duration: 12 weeks  ·  NDA — metrics aggregated

The Problem

A B2B productivity platform had invested 14 months building an advanced analytics suite. Usage data at 6 months post-launch: 4% of eligible users had activated the feature set. Sales team was field-explaining features that the product itself had failed to surface. The behavioral question: why were features invisible to the people who needed them most?

Research Methods

18 customer interviews (power users + non-activators), behavioral clickstream comparison analysis, in-product journey mapping, 4 moderated activation sessions with non-adopters. Focus: the gap between feature awareness, comprehension, and first use.

Behavioral Diagnoses

Discovery Failure

Feature was buried 3 navigation levels deep, accessible only through a workflow users had no reason to enter until already engaged with the feature.

Value Comprehension Gap

Onboarding copy described what the feature did technically, not what behavioral outcome it produced for the user's specific role context.

First-Use Anxiety

Feature required 7 configuration steps before producing any output. No scaffolded first-use or quick-win experience to demonstrate value before asking for setup investment.

Interventions Designed

Contextual surfacing: Designed behavioral triggers that surfaced the analytics suite at the moment users encountered the workflow problem it solved — in-context, not in a navigation menu.

Role-based value framing: Rewrote all feature introduction copy organized by role outcomes, not feature capabilities. "See which accounts need your attention this week" vs. "Access account analytics dashboard."

Scaffolded first-use flow: Built a 2-minute guided activation that produced a personalized insight before any configuration — value-first, setup-second architecture.

Measured Impact (NDA — aggregated metrics)

+38% feature activation rate  ·  +19% expansion revenue from activated accounts  ·  Time-to-first-value reduced from 23 minutes → 4 minutes  ·  Support volume for feature dropped 61%
